Foundations of GLM and Opus

What Is GLM?

  • Origin: Developed by the Beijing Academy of AI; debuted in 2022 as a bilingual (Chinese-English) transformer.
  • Architecture: A decoder-only design using rotary positional embeddings and a 4,096 token context window.
  • Training Data: 1.5 trillion tokens from web crawls, academic papers, and curated dialogues.
  • Core Capabilities:
    • High-fidelity code generation.
    • Robust multi-turn conversation handling.
    • Dominance in Chinese language benchmarks.

What Is Opus?

  • Development History: Launched by the European AI Lab (EAI) in 2023 to prioritize multilingualism and extended context.
  • Underlying Technology: A hybrid encoder-decoder architecture featuring Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) layers for dynamic compute scaling.
  • Training Corpus: 2.2 trillion tokens, heavily weighted toward European languages, legal texts, and scientific literature.
  • Core Capabilities:
    • Superior long-form generation (up to 16k tokens).
    • Advanced alignment that suppresses hallucinations.
    • High-precision multilingual translation.

Architectural Deep Dive

Model Size & Parameter Count

Both families offer a spectrum of sizes:

  1. GLM Variants

    • GLM-6B – 6 billion parameters; ideal for rapid prototyping.
    • GLM-130B – 130 billion parameters; built for enterprise workloads.

  2. Opus Variants

    • Opus-7B – 7 billion parameters; optimized for low-latency inference.
    • Opus-70B – 70 billion parameters; uses MoE to deliver expert-level performance.

The MoE design in Opus-70B is the secret sauce. It activates only a subset of experts per token, slashing compute requirements without sacrificing quality.

Training Corpus & Data Quality

  • GLM relies on a diverse but less curated dataset, using basic deduplication and profanity filters.
  • Opus employs a multi-stage cleaning pipeline involving language detection, bias mitigation, and factuality checks ([EXTERNAL_LINK: EAI_Research_Paper]).

Opus generally earns higher safety scores. However, GLM’s open-source nature allows developers to inject their own curated data.

Both models support parameter-efficient techniques that cut GPU memory consumption by up to 80%.

Inference Efficiency

  • Hardware Requirements:

    • GLM-130B requires 8 × A100 80 GB for real-time serving.
    • Opus-70B runs on 4 × A100 80 GB due to MoE sparsity.

  • Benchmark Summary (averaged over 1M tokens):

    1. Latency – Opus-7B: 30 ms, GLM-6B: 45 ms.
    2. Throughput – Opus-70B: 340 tokens/s, GLM-130B: 260 tokens/s.

Speed-hungry organizations should lean toward Opus. Those demanding total control over the weights should choose GLM.

Cost & Deployment Considerations

Licensing Models & Pricing

  • GLM – Apache-2.0. Free for commercial use; you pay for infrastructure only.
  • Opus – Commercial license. Pricing starts at $0.025 USD per 1k tokens on GCP, with volume discounts after 10M tokens.

A startup processing 5M tokens/month spends roughly $100 on Opus versus $40 on GLM (excluding hardware).

Frequently Asked Questions

Q1: How does glm opus compare in multilingual support?

A: Both support 30+ languages. Opus’s extended context and larger corpus give it a 4-6% BLEU advantage on low-resource pairs like Swahili-English.

Q2: Which model has lower inference cost per 1,000 tokens?

A: GLM-6B costs $\approx$ $0.02 USD per 1k tokens on AWS; Opus-7B averages $\approx$ $0.025 USD on GCP. GLM is cheaper for high-volume workloads.

Q3: Are there any open-source alternatives to Opus with comparable safety?

A: Yes. The Safety-GLM fork (Apache-2.0) adds Opus-style alignment layers, hitting a 0.82 safety score—close to Opus’s 0.89.

Q4: Can I fine-tune Opus on a single GPU?

A: Yes. Opus-7B fits on one NVIDIA A100 (40 GB) using LoRA adapters. Larger variants require multi-GPU pipelines or gradient checkpointing.

Q5: What are the licensing implications for commercial deployment?

A: GLM’s Apache-2.0 license is unrestricted. Opus’s commercial license is royalty-free up to 5M tokens/month, then charges usage fees. Check the EULA for revenue-share clauses.
